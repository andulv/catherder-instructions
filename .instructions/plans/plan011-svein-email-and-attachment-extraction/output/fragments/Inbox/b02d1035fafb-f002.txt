Hi Anders,

Frans and I have talked things through after your email, and we really like the direction you’re suggesting in alternative 2. We both feel that the best way forward is to work closely together on THC – The Hardware Company (Frans’ suggestion that is better than Drizzle 2.0) rather than keeping it at arm’s length.

If things go well and we enjoy the ride, we’d like to end up as near-equal partners — something like 35 : 35 : 30 or thereabouts. The exact numbers matter less than the idea of being roughly equal, with a small skew toward Frans and me since we’ve carried the foundation from Drizzle.

We’ve roughly logged 1,200 hours each on Gandalf since January 2023, plus covered expenses through Napkin Design. That gives a sense of scale for what’s gone in so far. The outline below aims to balance that while recognizing the extra push you can bring in the coming phase.

1. Trial & Startup Phase

Let’s start with a 6–12 month period where we work side by side and get THC properly off the ground.
Main goals:

  *   Develop the business plan, end-game scenarios, and sales material.
  *   Secure first sales and early customers / beta users.
  *   Handle the practicalities — payments, T&Cs, logistics, etc.
  *   Attend one or more fairs to test market traction.
  *   Set up small-scale in-house manufacturing.
  *   Cut costs through focused sourcing of components.
  *   Get the Raspberry Pi Pico software production-ready.
  *   Most importantly, see how we actually work together day-to-day.

If the collaboration feels right, we formalize the company setup after this phase.

2. Building Toward Equal Partnership

We’d like to reach near-equal ownership once we’ve all put in a comparable level of time and resources.
Since you’ll likely contribute more hours in the short term (and perhaps some capital), your share can build up gradually as we hit agreed milestones — for instance, finishing the Pico software, shipping the first production units, or closing the first sales.
This keeps things fair and flexible instead of locking the structure too early.

3. Keeping It Fair for Everyone

To protect all sides, we suggest a few simple principles:

  *   We each earn ownership gradually as we contribute.
  *   There’s a grace period (about a year) where, if one of us steps away or the fit isn’t right, no shares are kept.
  *   After that, ownership grows steadily over time until we reach the agreed target.
  *   If someone leaves later, the others can buy back their shares at a fair value so everyone can move on cleanly.

This isn’t red tape — just a practical way to stay fair and keep friendships intact.

4. Roles & Working Style

We should be upfront about task distribution. Some things none of us will love, but they still need doing. Let’s map those early and divide them evenly. Better to talk it through now than later.

5. Next Step

Lets have a video call soon to discuss where we are at and how to proceed:

  *   what the trial phase looks like in practice,
  *   key milestones and ownership steps
  *   some simple rules for how we work and communicate.

Best,
Peter & Frans

My role - Alternative 1
This is based on my interpretation of your offer in e-mail below:

  *   Investor & Software Subcontractor
  *   10% ownership.
  *   Probably board member.
  *   Pays for shares in cash and software work.
My lowest hourly rate for long-term projects is DKK 763,- (NOK 1200,-). This is the same price I used 8 years ago, so I believe it is a very fair price.

Wild guesstimate of hours needed to port software to Pi Pico and getting it into production: 400 hours
(I love to say half of that estimate but all experience tells me that hours runs quickly when end-user facing project goes into production, especially when going to production means shupping "firmware").

So my initial investment would be:
DKK 100 000,- in cash
DKK 305 200,- in software hours (400 hours * DKK 763)

As an potential minority-investor I need answer to the same questions that I believe every other potential investors will ask:

What is your plan for making this investment worthwhile?

  *   How much return will I receive on this investment.
  *   Will I receive dividends? If so when do you expect to start paying dividents and how much should I expect?
  *   Do you plan for an exit? If so, when and how much. And to whom?
  *   How much further investments is expected from me? For how long? (free software hours, further rounds with other investors)
  *   What will happen with my 10% when/if another investor comes aboard. Will mye shares be dilluted?
I believe an early-stage investor in a such a project would expect at least 10x return in 5-10 years.

I know that at this stage you are not ready to give answers to these questions. And it is hard for me to indicate if I intend to accept or reject the offer.

If we intend to for something similar to this alternative, where I am a minority investor, you need to come up with some form of plan that gives answers to these questions.

As an 10% owner I probably will be interested in being a board member, but I am reluctant to put in much unpaid work in form of business- and product-planning.
